Periodic position & electronic structure

Group 1 metals lie in the first column of the periodic table and have one outer electron. They readily lose that electron to form M⁺ ions. This explains many trends: low ionisation energy, increased reactivity down the group, and formation of ionic compounds.

Trends (down the group)

  • Atomic radius increases
  • Ionisation energy decreases
  • Reactivity increases
  • Melting points generally fall
  • Density generally increases (Li→Fr)

Exam tip

Always link reactivity to how easily the outer electron can be removed — shielding and distance from the nucleus are key.

Physical properties

  • Soft metals — can be cut with a knife. Freshly cut surfaces are shiny but tarnish in air.
  • Low density: Li, Na and K float on water due to low density and large atomic volume.
  • Poorly hard compared with transition metals; lower melting points than many metals.
  • Good conductors of heat and electricity.

Physical tests & observations

Flame tests and simple density/appearance observations are often used in labs. Flame test colours: Li — crimson; Na — intense yellow; K — lilac.

Chemical properties (core Y10–11 content)

Key chemical behaviours of alkali metals:

  • With water (cold): 2M(s) + 2H₂O(l) → 2MOH(aq) + H₂(g). Observations increase down group: gentle fizzing → violent reaction, hydrogen release and possible flames.
  • With oxygen: Form oxides/peroxides/superoxides depending on metal and conditions. Example: 4Li + O₂ → 2Li₂O.
  • With halogens (chlorine): Vigorous formation of ionic salts: 2M + Cl₂ → 2MCl (white crystalline ionic solids).
  • Hydroxides (MOH): Strong bases; dissolve to give OH⁻ — strong alkalinity.
  • Reducing behaviour: Alkali metals are powerful reducing agents; they donate electrons easily to reduce other species.

Example balanced equations

  • 2Li(s) + 2H₂O(l) → 2LiOH(aq) + H₂(g)
  • 2Na(s) + 2H₂O(l) → 2NaOH(aq) + H₂(g)
  • 4K(s) + O₂(g) → 2K₂O(s)
  • 2Na(s) + Cl₂(g) → 2NaCl(s)

Exam prep — worked examples & quick quiz

Quick quiz

Exam-style questions

  1. Describe what you see when sodium is dropped into cold water. (3 marks)
  2. Write equations for: (a) 2Li + 2H₂O → ? (b) 2Na + Cl₂ → ?
  3. A student reacts 0.46 g Na — calculate the volume of H₂ at RTP (24 dm³ mol⁻¹).
Model answers
  1. Float, fizzes, melts into a ball, hydrogen gas produced, orange flame sometimes, forms colourless NaOH solution.
  2. (a) 2Li + 2H₂O → 2LiOH + H₂; (b) 2Na + Cl₂ → 2NaCl
  3. n(Na)=0.46/23=0.02 mol → H₂ = 0.01 mol → volume = 0.01×24 = 0.24 dm³ (240 cm³).

Glossary

Alkali

Soluble base producing OH⁻ in water (e.g., NaOH).

Ionisation energy

Energy to remove an electron from an atom in the gas phase.

Shielding

Inner electrons reduce nuclear attraction on outer electrons.
