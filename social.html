1.1 Periodic Position & Electronic Structure

Group 1 metals are the first column of the periodic table. They all have one outer electron, so they form +1 ions (M⁺) and similar compounds such as chlorides (MCl), oxides (M₂O), and hydroxides (MOH).

Trend Highlights

  • Atomic radius increases down the group.
  • Ionisation energy decreases down the group.
  • Reactivity increases down the group.
  • Melting/boiling points generally decrease down the group.
  • Density generally increases down the group (Li, Na, K float).

Exam Tip

Link reactivity to the outer electron being further from the nucleus and more shielded, so it’s easier to lose.

1.2 Physical Properties

  • Soft metals (cut with a knife); lustrous when freshly cut, tarnish in air.
  • Low density (Li, Na, K float on water).
  • Good electrical/thermal conductors.
  • Low melting points compared with most other metals.

2.1 With Water (Cold)

General reaction: 2M(s) + 2H₂O(l) → 2MOH(aq) + H₂(g)

Observations (increasing down group)

  • Lithium — floats, fizzes gently; forms colourless LiOH.
  • Sodium — floats, melts into a ball, fizzes more; may produce orange flame briefly.
  • Potassium — vigorous, lilac flame; may produce sparks; forms KOH rapidly.

Safety

  • Use small pieces with tongs; safety screens; eye protection.
  • Never handle with wet hands; keep a class-appropriate risk assessment.
  • Have dry sand (not water) for extinguishing.
Worked example: Calculate moles of H₂ from 0.23 g Na

Moles Na = 0.23 / 23.0 = 0.010 mol. From 2Na : 1H₂, moles H₂ = 0.010 / 2 = 0.0050 mol.

2.2 With Oxygen (Air)

Form oxides/peroxides/superoxides (depends on metal & conditions). Simple model: 4Li + O₂ → 2Li₂O, 4Na + O₂ → 2Na₂O (and sometimes Na₂O₂).

Freshly cut surface tarnishes quickly due to oxide layer formation.

2.3 With Halogens

Vigorous to form ionic halides: 2M + Cl₂ → 2MCl

White crystalline salts (e.g., NaCl, KCl) with high melting points, soluble in water, conduct electricity when molten or in solution.

2.4 Flame Tests

  • Lithium — crimson red
  • Sodium — intense yellow
  • Potassium — lilac
  • Rubidium — red-violet (beyond basic spec but good to know)
  • Cesium — blue-violet (beyond basic spec)

3.1 Applications

  • Lithium: rechargeable batteries, alloys, greases.
  • Sodium: street lighting (legacy), chemicals, glass making, NaOH production.
  • Potassium: fertilisers (K⁺ essential for plants), KOH in soaps/alkaline cells.

Storage & Handling

  • Store under oil or inert atmosphere to prevent oxidation.
  • Use non-aqueous extinguishing methods (dry sand) — never water on alkali metal fires.
  • Follow local lab safety SOPs and hazard codes.

3.2 Solubility & Alkalinity

Hydroxides (MOH) are strong alkalis; form strongly alkaline solutions (high pH), fully ionise to give OH⁻.

4.1 Common Misconceptions

  • “Reactivity decreases down Group 1.” — False. It increases because the outer electron is further from the nucleus and more shielded.
  • “All metals sink in water.” — False. Li, Na, K float (low density).
  • “Add water to put out sodium fires.” — Dangerous. Use dry sand or Class D agent.

4.2 Quick Quiz

4.3 Exam-Style Questions

  1. (Short) Describe what you would see when a small piece of sodium is added to cold water. Include at least three observations.
  2. (Structured) Write balanced symbol equations for:
    • (a) lithium with water
    • (b) sodium with chlorine
    • (c) potassium with oxygen (assume oxide)
  3. (Application) A student reacts 0.46 g of sodium with excess water. Calculate the volume of hydrogen produced at RTP (24 dm³ mol⁻¹). Show your working.
Suggested Answers (Reveal)
  1. Sodium floats and moves around; melts into a silvery ball; fizzing/bubbling; may show an orange flame briefly; forms a colourless solution.
  2. (a) 2Li(s) + 2H₂O(l) → 2LiOH(aq) + H₂(g)
    (b) 2Na(s) + Cl₂(g) → 2NaCl(s)
    (c) 4K(s) + O₂(g) → 2K₂O(s)
  3. n(Na) = 0.46 / 23.0 = 0.020 mol
    2Na : 1H₂ → n(H₂) = 0.020 / 2 = 0.010 mol
    Volume H₂ = 0.010 × 24 = 0.24 dm³ (240 cm³)

Glossary (Tap to expand)

Alkali

A soluble base that produces OH⁻ ions in aqueous solution (e.g., NaOH, KOH).

Ionisation Energy

Energy required to remove an electron from a gaseous atom; decreases down Group 1.

Shielding

Inner electrons reduce the effective nuclear attraction on the outer electron.

Superoxide

Compound containing the O₂⁻ ion (e.g., KO₂ under specific conditions).
