Introduction to Alkanes

Alkanes are the simplest hydrocarbons. They are saturated because they contain only single covalent bonds. Examples include methane (CH₄), ethane (C₂H₆) and propane (C₃H₈).

General Formula

The general formula for alkanes is CₙH₂ₙ₊₂. Example: For n = 4, the formula is C₄H₁₀ (butane).

Naming Alkanes

Alkanes are named using the root for the number of carbons plus "-ane". For branched alkanes, find the longest chain, number it, and name branches accordingly.

Isomerism

Isomers have the same molecular formula but different structures. Example: Butane has n-butane and isobutane.

Physical Properties

Alkanes are non-polar and do not mix with water. Their boiling points increase with chain length, and branching lowers boiling points.

Combustion

Complete combustion produces CO₂ and H₂O. Incomplete combustion produces carbon monoxide or soot.

Uses of Alkanes

Alkanes are used as fuels (gas, petrol) and in products like candles and lubricants.

Environmental Impact

Burning alkanes produces CO₂ which contributes to global warming. Incomplete combustion can produce poisonous CO gas.
