Physical properties (Year 10 clarity)

  • Appearance: Soft, shiny metals when freshly cut (silvery). They tarnish in air.
  • Density: Generally low for the first three (Li, Na, K). Li, Na, K can float on water due to low density.
  • Melting points: Relatively low compared with typical metals; generally decrease down the group (with variations).
  • Conductivity: Conduct heat and electricity (typical metal behaviour).

Chemical properties (Year 10 clarity)

Short, clear statements students must remember:

  • Reactivity: Reactivity increases down Group 1 (outer electron easier to lose).
  • With water: Produce hydroxide (alkaline solution) and hydrogen gas. Balanced equation: 2M(s) + 2H₂O(l) → 2MOH(aq) + H₂(g).
  • With oxygen: Form oxides/peroxides (depends on metal and conditions); simple oxide equation: 4M + O₂ → 2M₂O (example for Li/Na in basic teaching).
  • With chlorine: Form ionic chlorides: 2M + Cl₂ → 2MCl. These are white ionic salts (e.g., NaCl).
  • Hydroxides: MOH are strong alkalis (high pH) and fully dissociate in water.

Key equations & worked examples

Common balanced equations

  • 2Li(s) + 2H₂O(l) → 2LiOH(aq) + H₂(g)
  • 2Na(s) + 2H₂O(l) → 2NaOH(aq) + H₂(g)
  • 4K(s) + O₂(g) → 2K₂O(s)
  • 2Na(s) + Cl₂(g) → 2NaCl(s)

Worked example (Year 10)

Calculate the volume of hydrogen produced when 0.46 g of sodium reacts with excess water at RTP (24.0 dm³ mol⁻¹).

  1. n(Na) = mass / Mr = 0.46 / 23.0 = 0.0200 mol
  2. From 2Na → H₂, n(H₂) = 0.0200 / 2 = 0.0100 mol
  3. Volume H₂ = n × 24.0 = 0.0100 × 24.0 = 0.240 dm³ = 240 cm³

Safety & teacher notes (short)

  • Perform reactions with appropriate risk assessment and protective screens in place.
  • Store alkali metals under oil to avoid contact with moisture/air.
  • For fires, use dry sand or Class D extinguishers — do not use water on large alkali metal fires.
  • Demonstrations should use tiny amounts and follow local policies.
